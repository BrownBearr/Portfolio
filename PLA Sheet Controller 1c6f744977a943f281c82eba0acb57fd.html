IDEA

Use sustainable materials to create a hitbox style controller.

Design it to withstand the mashing in a fighting game

Make it something I will use in the future

Inspiration

Inspired by the DIY nature of the fighting game community

Seen fightsticks made from everything from “xbox cases to shoe boxes”

Want to contribute to this endeavor by prototyping with a unique material

Inspired by the sustainability efforts of the NYU Makerspace and the RePlasticing class

Want to use melted PLA plastic scrap from 3D prints to create a new fightstick.

Enclosure Fabrication

This project included:

  1. Orange/White Sheet | For the base plate
  1. Blue/Black Sheet | Walls & Button Mounts
  1. Grey/White Marble Sheet | Top Control Panel

Each piece needed to be laser cut to certain dimensions

I designed the case to have a top and bottom plate held together by standoffs

Walls were later designed to house buttons and enclose all electronics

The final dimensions of the enclosure is 12in x 8in x 2in

Designed in reference to the MadCatz Street Fighter IV SE Fightstick dimensions

Screwholes in 4 corners to accomodate standoffs

5mm wide gaps to accomodate PLA walls

14 24mm buttons & 1 30mm button holes based on Sanwa buttons

With these files, I began to laser cut the sheets

The Laser Cutter used was the Epilog Fusion Laser

The Laser Settings used in these cuts were the following:

  1. 5 Speed | 100 Power | 80 Frequency
  1. 30 Speed | 100 Power | 80 Frequency

The laser would make a first pass to cut out the design and burn through all the PLA materials

When PLA is cut in the laser cutter, the laser melts the edges and if not removed, the PLA will begin to bond with itself again after cooling

The second pass is then used to quickly trim off any PLA that has bonded together so that the user can then quickly remove excess material and break off any bonded pieces before they fully set in place.

The orange sheet sides were burned due to a miscut and incorrect calibration on my part

Wiring and Electronics

This is where the bulk of my time went towards.

Instead of buying precrimped, quick release wires and a full PCB, I opted for a homebrew route

I bought a Rasberry Pi Pico and loaded it with FeralAI and Sleepunits GP2040 Firmware for easy microcontroller to gamepad mapping. Link below:

I then used a protoboard and screw terminals which were graciously given to me by David Rios.

Thank you David Rios

I soldered both the Pi Pico and Screw Terminals to the Proto board and began to solder wires between the seperate pins and the screw terminals

Now that the Protoboard had been prepped and was able to transmit signals from the pins to the screw terminals, I began to solder wires to the buttons

I began by first creating a daisy chain between all the buttons for ground

After linking all buttons to ground, I began to painstakingly individually solder a wire to each button

After 6 hours of soldering, I had finally completed all of the button soldering

While my soldering job was not perfect, it did lead to some problems with shorting. Luckily I was able to separate the board and the buttons enough to not create and shorting.

I finally closed the bottom plate and the build in complete
